How I think about design

To me, design is communication, and as a product designer, I use design to solve problems in digital products.

Every problem is different, but most have three parts: 1) user needs 2) business needs and 3) existing systems. Projects start with knowing only slivers of each part, and coming up with a good solution requires filling out as many unknowns as possible. It's tempting to solve for only one part, but a good design makes the right tradeoffs to satisfy all three.

Designers each have a tool belt they use to accomplish this. We have access to volumes of user research methods, scales to weigh resources against top-line metrics, and extensive dictionary entries for concepts like scalability, cannibalization, network effects, signal gathering, and feasibility. We leverage our creativity, understanding of human behavior, and visual interaction chops to sort, organize and finally, land on a solution.

Of these tools, I value tool belt grooming, a.k.a. validating and learning from our work, the most. Validation happens in many forms, from usability testing to experimentation to internal/external feedback. Once we can validate, and ideally isolate, what were the exact pieces that solved the problem, we can tuck that learning into our tool belt and hopefully, reproduce that same success for the next problem.

My life story

I discovered the world of design when I took a course on graphical user interfaces while studying Architecture at MIT. Being low-key fascinated by languages and how communication works, I decided to pivot and pursue a career in design, which I saw as a visual form of communication.

After graduating in 2014, I moved to Tokyo, Japan to design sparkly mobile apps for the Disney Interactive group (and learn Japanese). After 1.5 years of eating ramen, I moved to the home base of the design industry: San Francisco. I was swept up by startup culture and joined VenueNext where I worked on B2B management solutions for event venues.

After another 1.5 years, I came back to consumer products at Quora. Nowadays, I think about how to distribute content and drive content growth.
