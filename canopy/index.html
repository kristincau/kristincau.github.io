About the Project

VenueNext integrates services like wayfinding, POS and ticketing to transform how guests experience a venue, from arenas and concert halls to hotels and hospitals.

I sought to redesign Canopy, a venue management tool built and designed by the engineering team. Hearing about its severe lack in usability, I kicked off the project by interviewing internal users, customers, and stakeholders. From my interview learnings and comprehensive audit, I restructured the product’s information architecture, introduced new UX patterns, and defined a more appropriate visual design system.

The Product

Canopy takes the third party services used in a venue, such as the point of sale system, location, ticketing and loyalty program, and integrates them into a single platform. Canopy is the control center to operate these services. VenueNext employees use Canopy to set up, maintain and test systems, while venue managers are use it to maintain, customize and control their organization.

The Big Picture

As an enterprise tool, this web app targets internal VenueNext Employees, and external Venue Managers.

Canopy was created for venues to manage their own systems based on their unique business goals. Despite the tool’s extensive functionality, customers were still asking VenueNext employees to help set up their organizations. This was a clear indicator that customers weren’t using Canopy as intended. The issue boiled down to these fundamentals:

  1. Lack of product maintenance

    2. A content audit revealed a whopping 118 unique pages (and growing!), many of which were either forgotten or had an unknown purpose

  2. Lack of customer empathy

    3. Features were built based on the convenience of engineering timelines, which meant usability took a huge hit

  3. Unclear platform direction and product goals

    4. Integrating multiple services from multiple third parties left our platform spread thin across disparate goals

The Magic of Tidying Up

The most effective step to take was to re-architect the entire product. In order to achieve this, it required a full product audit, direct collaboration with current users, and insight on stakeholder goals. While unique customer needs were taken into account, decisions were made based on goals common across all customers. Doing so would allow for scalability and safeguards against deteriorating again.

To re-architect the pages, my approach was simple but comprehensive. The audit revealed that Canopy consisted mainly of different objects, each of which had actionable or editable attributes. Following this logic, a Canopy section would list objects of the same type (ex: food and beverage items, distribution channels) and have unique accompanying details page for each object that provided any necessary customization options.

Additionally, to provide context and clarify an object’s attributes, an object can be previewed from a listings page by selecting on it.

New Year, New Look

Canopy was originally built using the Bootstrap framework. Given the redesign, however, the new design started from scratch. Working in parallel on App Builder[link here], I aligned the two visual systems to strengthen brand consistency.

The previous layout was riddled with data runoff and forced horizontal scrolling, a major pain point for the many Internet Explorer users. To solve this, a filtering system that allows users to choose what data they want to see about an object was introduced in tables that were prone to have too much information.

The existing typeface, Proxima Nova, proved to be too wide making it inappropriate for displaying large amounts of tabular data. It was replaced with the open source Open Sans.

A misuse of a bright orange being used for all buttons and links muddled the visual hierarchy and made it confusing as to what on a page was important. Maintaining the previous blue-orange color scheme for brand consistency, blue tints were introduced.

Additionally, rather than using the accent orange as the sole call-to-action color, a new set of more subtle buttons were introduced.

Product in the Wild

As a live product, Canopy releases must be done thoughtfully and carefully. To accommodate the full redesign, a considerable engineering effort of switching the product from Ruby on Rails to React is required. Initial releases focus on re-organizing the tool, which requires a back end re-architecture, while future releases will focus on visual renewals.
