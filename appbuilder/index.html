About the Project

VenueNext integrates services like wayfinding, POS and ticketing to transform how guests experience a venue, from arenas and concert halls to hotels and hospitals.

I was tasked to rethink the pre-existing, but soon to be obsolete, App Builder web tool. App Builder harnesses the flexibility of the VenueNext internal mobile front-end framework to control the UI of an application based on time and location. I gained firsthand experience of customer pain points and use cases, designed the flow, learned and documented the front-end framework, conducted user testing, outlined the feature set, and built the visual system.

The Product

App Builder let Venues customize their app’s UI based on the time and the location of a user. Clients can choose from a library of pre-made UI building blocks, called widgets, to put in their application. Each widget comes with specific types of customizations such as text display, color, or image. Once happy with their changes, a client can preview their changes on a mobile device, or choose to publish their changes to their users.

The Big Picture

As an enterprise tool, this web app targets internal VenueNext Employees and external Venue Managers.

Our front end framework made possible UI changes in applications without a new release. A non-engineering VenueNext employee who understood JSON would be able to make color, text, layout, and even function edits to an app.

Due to the niche target of this product, I conducted research by first hand learning the front end framework, maintaining our mobile front-end, and working with the client services team to understand what type of changes were requested and how the company responded. Based on learnings, App Builder was designed with these goals in mind:

  1. Make clients responsible for their own apps

    2. Each client has their own set of visual needs. Clients should feel ownership over their applications and be able to make changes on their own.

  2. Eliminate human error with automation

    3. Manually editing the front-end is prone to human error and inefficiency. Changes are not tracked making error correction difficult.

  3. Reduce the burden on VenueNext employees

    4. With a growing number of clients, VenueNext does not have the resources to manage every client’s app UI.

  4. Provide an interface to hand off new features to clients

    5. There is no defined process for how VenueNext releases new features to clients. Clients hear of new features only when told by their respective liaison.

Blue Sky

I worked together with the lead engineer to define product requirements on a specialized team. Nonetheless, we had little insight on company priorities, overarching timelines, and stakeholder goals. We iterated through high level product flows, hand sketches, low fidelity wireframes, high fidelity wireframes and visual designs.

Our goal was to build a visual representation of our front end framework. The task at hand could be equated to building a tool that outputs HTML/CSS based off some visual composition. Realizing that this type of project was both a technical overcommitment and our clients didn’t necessarily have need for such a robust tool, we knew we needed to prioritize our releases.

We decided our first release would allow limited customization of our most basic widgets and show a real-time rendering of the mobile UI on desktop. While the complexity of our framework set constraints and put requirements on the design, the team believed that defining a single visual system would help set a precedent for the tool.

Reorienting the Product

After 9 months of working on the tool, the company shifted from an agency approach to a product-as-a-platform approach that was more scalable and sustainable. Equipped with a new product team, the company had a renewed and immediate focus on App Builder.

The team needed to make serious adjustments and simplifications to meet a new, earlier release date. We had to cut the tool down to the bare bones: what does it need to just be useful? We removed the real-time rendering, replacing it with representational UI, and removed widgets, replacing it with widget templates. While the idea of widget templates existed in the original product design, it was now put at the forefront.

Making Sense of Visuals

Since VenueNext lacked both a strong brand voice and an established visual system, the design of this enterprise tool began on a blank slate. Working in parallel on Canopy[link here], I aligned the two visual systems to establish brand consistency.

Given the complexity of the tool, it was important to make the visuals clean and simple. I decided to use a dark UI over a light UI to give the app the “sandbox feeling” that is often associated web tools for customization.

To resolve the dichotomy of actions that can be performed for App Builder versus actions that can be performed for the mobile app, I devised a color hierarchy. In the example of buttons, while primary and secondary buttons have similar styling, blue indicate actions performed on App Builder, while green indicated actions performed on the venue’s app.

For a visual affirmation of brand consistency, I designed a set of line icons. By using a similar motif, I could establish a relationship between the different types of widgets, which was an unfamiliar concept to our users.

It's a Marathon, Not a Race

With a beta release to a single client, the development of App Builder is still in progress. Monthly releases of the tool will be released in order of customer need.
