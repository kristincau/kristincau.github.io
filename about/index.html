On Design

I care a lot about the big picture. Before diving into the nitty gritty of design, it’s always worth thinking about whether or not the problem we’re trying to solve is the right problem(s) to solve. While good design takes into account usability and aesthetics, great design will also take into account business goals and technical constraints. At a high level, my design process goes something like this:

  1. Learn from everybody and everything

    2. Understanding users’ situations paints part of the picture, but not all of it. Talking to stakeholders and engineers, taking stock of what has been done thus far, and analyzing the products and market strategies of competitors, will help to avoid surprises down the road.

  2. Process the learnings

    3. This might take the form of sketches, a mind map, wireframes, or even a prototype. The key here is to filter and combine the learnings into something visual and tangible. Filtering might take a few sub steps depending on the type of information gathered.

  3. Take what you have and go back to Step 1

    4. Running with your head down might be faster, but it comes at the risk of not ending at the finish line. At appropriate intervals, the visual and tangible something that came out of Step 2 should be taken back to Step 1. This might mean an internal review, user testing, or gathering more data.

Setting a design process in stone can be dangerous. Just as user empathy in a design is important, empathy for project collaborators is just as important. Every project is different and a design process should be flexible enough to accommodate a given situation.
